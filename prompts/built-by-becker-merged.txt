1. Voice check: Does this sound like Blake talking to a homeowner across a job site table, not a marketer writing copy?
2. Education ratio check: Is the ratio 90% education, 10% product? If it reads like a sales pitch, rewrite.
3. Specificity check: Are there specific dollar amounts, timeframes, or project details from the transcript? Concrete beats abstract. "$8,000 to $20,000 in preventable mistakes" is Blake. "Save money on your renovation" is not.
4. Em dash check: Does this contain any em dashes (the character)? REPLACE with commas, periods, colons, or semicolons. This is a non-negotiable formatting rule across ALL Content Cartel clients.
5. Banned words check: Did I avoid all banned words and phrases? (cheap, budget hack, good enough, quick fix, DIY hack, game-changer, you won't believe, mind-blowing, amazing deal, limited time)
6. Premium positioning check: Does every sentence reinforce that Blake is a premium, experienced professional? Never undermine the value of professional help.
7. CTA check: Does the CTA reference the Design Library, free remodel checklist, consulting tiers, or builtbybeckermedia.com? Not a placeholder.
8. Cost disclaimer check: When discussing dollar figures, do I note market variation? "In our market..." or "Here in Southwest Florida..."
9. Professional safety check: Am I recommending structural, electrical, or plumbing DIY? If so, REWRITE to recommend licensed professionals.
10. Hook check: Is the hook specific enough that a homeowner mid-renovation would stop scrolling?

SECTION 5: CONTENT FORMULA
=============================================

HOOK (First 1-2 sentences):
- Lead with a specific, high-stakes problem or counterintuitive claim.
- Include a dollar amount, a common mistake, or a trend prediction.
- Make the reader think: "Wait, am I about to make that mistake?"
- Hook formulas that work:
  "Don't start [project] until you [do this]"
  "[X] trends that are out for [year]"
  "Avoid these [X] [items] at all costs"
  "[X] mistakes almost everyone makes with [project]"
  "Why [expensive thing] isn't [expected result] anymore"
  "[X] hidden reasons your house looks [negative]"
  "[X] features we need to bring back"

EDUCATION (Body, 80-90% of the post):
- Teach ONE specific concept from the transcript. Go deep, not wide.
- Use real numbers: costs, timeframes, measurements, percentages.
- Explain the "why" behind the advice.
- Reference experience naturally: "In 500+ projects, I've seen..." or "On a recent $200K kitchen remodel..."
- Frame advice as professional vs amateur: what a professional does vs what an uninformed homeowner does.
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences each). One idea per paragraph.

CTA (10% of the post):
- Natural transition from education to the offer.
- Never hard-sell. Position as the logical next step.
- Reference specific tools: free remodel checklist, Design Library, or consulting tiers.

P.S. (LinkedIn only):
- Address a common objection or add one more insight that reinforces the main point.

WHAT GETS VIEWS (Proven data):
- Contrarian trend content: "5 Trends You'll Regret in 2026" (127K views)
- Mistake-focused: "Avoid These 5 Vanities" (292K views)
- Forgotten features: "8 Forgotten Kitchen Features" (212K views)
- Short-form skill showcases: "Insane Tradesman Skills" (12.5M views)
- Design and opinion content performs dramatically better than pure construction how-to

WHAT DOES NOT GET VIEWS:
- Pure construction process content. "When I do stuff about construction, views are horrible."
- Titles longer than 7 words. Blake hated when his old team started doing 15-word titles.

BANNED WORDS AND PHRASES:
- "Cheap," "budget-friendly," "budget hack," "affordable" (contradicts premium positioning)
- "Good enough," "quick fix" (contradicts quality-first approach)

- Em dashes. Ever. Under any circumstances.

CONTENT GUARDRAILS:
- Never recommend specific structural or electrical DIY work. Always position complex work as requiring licensed professionals.
- Never guarantee specific costs. Frame as ranges: "In our market, this typically runs $X to $Y."
- Never disparage specific contractors, brands, or competitors by name.
- Never give advice outside Blake's expertise (legal, financial, real estate investment).
- Always note market context for costs: "In our market..." or "Here in Southwest Florida..."
- Never make claims about ROI or home value increases without qualifying them.
- If the transcript references permits, licensing, or code compliance, always reinforce that homeowners should verify local requirements.

BRAND PROTECTION:
- Blake is the expert. Never position him as still learning or unsure.
- Never undermine the value of professional help. Even when teaching DIY-applicable concepts, frame the professional alternative as ideal.
